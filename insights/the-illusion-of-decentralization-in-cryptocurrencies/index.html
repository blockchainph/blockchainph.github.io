The Illusion of Decentralization in Cryptocurrencies

By Ferdie James Nervida
Founder, Blockchain Practitioners Association of the Philippines (BPAP)

Summary

Decentralization is often described as the defining feature of cryptocurrencies. However, the systems that support blockchain networks, such as internet infrastructure, power supply, and hardware manufacturing, remain highly centralized. Understanding these dependencies helps practitioners develop a more realistic view of how decentralized systems operate in the real world.

Cryptocurrencies are frequently described as decentralized systems that remove the need for banks or central authorities. Bitcoin, the first and most widely known cryptocurrency, was designed to allow peer-to-peer transactions where trust is placed in code and distributed networks rather than intermediaries.

But when we look more closely at how these systems function in practice, an interesting paradox appears. While blockchain protocols may be decentralized, the infrastructure that makes them possible is often not.

For blockchain practitioners, it is useful to look beyond the protocol layer and consider the broader systems that support these networks.

The Hidden Centralization Triad

Three major dependencies shape how cryptocurrency networks operate: internet connectivity, power supply, and hardware manufacturing. Each introduces potential points of concentration that influence how decentralized a network truly is.

Internet Infrastructure

Blockchain nodes must communicate continuously across the internet in order to validate and propagate transactions. While the internet itself was originally designed as a decentralized network, access to it is typically controlled by a limited number of Internet Service Providers (ISPs) in each country.

In addition, the physical backbone of the internet, including data centers, fiber networks, and undersea cables, is concentrated in specific locations. Disruptions to these infrastructures, whether caused by technical issues, natural disasters, or regulatory actions, can affect the connectivity of blockchain networks.

The Power Paradox

Cryptocurrency networks also rely heavily on electricity. This is particularly evident in Proof-of-Work systems such as Bitcoin, where mining requires significant computational power.

Mining operations naturally move to regions where electricity is cheaper or more abundant. Over time, this creates geographic clusters of mining activity. When a large share of the network’s hash power is concentrated in certain areas, the system becomes more sensitive to local regulations, infrastructure failures, or energy policies.

The Silicon Bottleneck

Another important factor is hardware. Cryptocurrency mining and validation often depend on specialized computing equipment such as ASIC miners or high-performance GPUs.

The global semiconductor industry is concentrated among a relatively small number of manufacturers. This creates supply chain dependencies that can influence how widely mining hardware is distributed and who has access to it.

Looking Beyond the Protocol

These dependencies are not unique to Bitcoin. Many blockchain networks, regardless of their consensus mechanisms, rely on the same underlying infrastructure layers: telecommunications networks, power grids, and semiconductor manufacturing.

For practitioners, this highlights an important point: decentralization is not determined solely by how a protocol is designed. It is shaped by the broader technological and economic systems that support it.

A More Realistic View of Decentralization

The cryptocurrency community continues to explore ways to reduce these dependencies. Some initiatives include:

  • mesh networking approaches that improve communication resilience
  • distributed and renewable energy solutions for mining operations
  • efforts to diversify hardware manufacturing

While promising, these approaches are still evolving and face practical challenges.

Understanding these infrastructure dependencies helps us move toward a more realistic view of decentralization. Rather than treating decentralization as an absolute state, it may be more accurate to see it as a spectrum shaped by both digital protocols and real-world systems.

For blockchain practitioners, recognizing this complexity is an important step in designing, building, and evaluating decentralized technologies.
